What Alternatives Are Not_

Before you invest in any alternative asset, a clear-eyed accounting of what they are not will serve you better than any enthusiasm about returns. The upside case for alternatives is well documented. This is the other side of that conversation.

A Fair Accounting_

Not fully liquid. Unlike stocks, you often can't exit on short notice. Some investments lock up your capital for 5–10 years. This is not a hidden risk — it is a structural feature of the asset class. Know it before you enter.

Not simple to evaluate. Valuing a private company or a piece of art requires knowledge and due diligence that public market investing doesn't demand. There is no live ticker. There is no consensus price. You are making a judgment call with incomplete information — the same as any sophisticated investor, but without the institutional support most of them have.

Not unregulated. Most professional alternative investment managers are registered with the SEC. But the registration exemptions that govern many alternative offerings — Regulation D, Rule 506(c) — mean these products receive less public scrutiny than registered securities. Due diligence responsibility shifts to you.

Not right for everyone at every stage. Alternatives typically make sense once you have an emergency fund, no high-interest debt, and some traditional market exposure. They are a complement to a financial foundation — not a replacement for one.

Most financial advisors suggest alternatives as 10–30% of a total portfolio, layered on top of traditional market exposure — not instead of it.

How to Think About Your Own Risk Tolerance_

Before investing in any alternative asset, honest answers to three questions will tell you most of what you need to know.

1. Time horizon. When might I need this money? If the answer is "within 5 years," most alternatives are not appropriate. Illiquidity is not a theoretical risk — it is a real constraint that will matter if your circumstances change.

2. Liquidity needs. Could I survive financially if this money was locked up and inaccessible for several years? If not, liquidity risk is too high regardless of the potential return.

3. Knowledge. Do I understand how this asset makes money, what could go wrong, and how I would exit? If not, more research is needed before investing. Enthusiasm is not due diligence.

What the Data Actually Says_

Private equity has outperformed public markets by approximately ~3% annually over a 20-year average. That is a meaningful premium. It is also earned over long time horizons, with capital that is genuinely illiquid, in vehicles that require a level of access and minimum commitment that has historically been out of reach for most individual investors.

The argument for learning about alternatives now — even if you don't invest in them yet — is that the landscape is changing. Regulatory updates, new technology platforms, and broader access structures are lowering the barriers. Understanding the asset class before you encounter it is what gives you the ability to evaluate it clearly when you do.

The Right Frame_

Alternative assets are not a shortcut. They are not a guaranteed premium. They are an expansion of the option set — available to those who understand what they are entering, what they are giving up, and why the tradeoff is worth it for their specific situation.
