## The Wealth Transfer Moment

The Great Wealth Transfer is already underway. Over the next two decades, an estimated $84 trillion in assets will move from Baby Boomers to Gen X, Millennials, and Gen Z — with women positioned to receive a disproportionate share through inheritance, divorce, and the deaths of male spouses who typically predecease them.

Women who receive this wealth face a specific challenge. They are often intelligent and accomplished in domains entirely unrelated to investing. They are taking on financial complexity — alternative assets, private equity funds, estate structures, tax implications — that they did not build and may not have chosen. They are meeting with advisors who assume background knowledge they do not yet have. And they are making decisions with permanence and consequence before they are ready.

This platform was built specifically for this moment. Not financial advice — education. The vocabulary, the frameworks, the behavioral self-awareness, and the questions to ask that make the difference between a productive advisory relationship and one where the client defers to expertise she cannot yet evaluate.

## Building Your Advisory Team After Wealth Transfer

Who you need and what they do:

**Investment Advisor (RIA — Registered Investment Advisor)** — Fiduciary, manages the investment strategy. The most important distinction: a fiduciary is legally required to act in your interest. A broker operating under the suitability standard is not. Ask explicitly: "Are you a fiduciary for all of my assets, at all times?"

**Estate Attorney** — Wills, trusts, beneficiary designations, powers of attorney, generational transfer structures. Essential immediately after any significant wealth event.

**Tax Advisor (CPA/CFP)** — Alternative asset tax implications are complex: K-1s from limited partnerships, step-up in cost basis for inherited assets, carried interest taxation, QSBS for startup founders. You need someone who understands these specifically.

**Family Office Consultant** — If the wealth is significant enough to warrant one, can help establish governance structures and an investment policy statement before you start deploying capital.

**Art Advisor** — If the inherited wealth includes a significant collection, an art advisor helps with provenance documentation, insurance valuation, collection strategy, and the decision of whether to donate, sell, or hold.

## The Behavioral Layer No One Talks About

Research consistently shows that sudden wealth — inherited or earned — triggers specific psychological patterns that traditional financial education does not address:

**Loss aversion amplified** — When the wealth is inherited rather than built, the emotional weight of "losing" it can be so acute that it leads to paralysis or extreme risk aversion that does not serve the portfolio.

**Impostor syndrome and financial confidence gaps** — Women in particular report feeling unqualified to make decisions about wealth they did not personally generate. This is a structural problem, not an individual one. The Gender and Behavioral Investing module addresses this directly.

**Anchoring to the donor** — Heirs frequently make investment decisions based on how the original wealth-holder would have decided, even when the circumstances, tax implications, and time horizon are completely different.

**Overconfidence after business success** — Female founders who sell companies often bring extraordinary domain expertise to the investment decision — which research shows can produce overconfidence in entirely different domains.

The Behavioral Economics module — based on Kahneman, Tversky, Thaler, and contemporary neuroscience — gives you the framework to recognize these patterns in yourself before they affect decisions that cannot be easily undone.
