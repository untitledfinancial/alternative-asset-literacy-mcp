Art as Investment 7 min read

Let this be a love letter to art, in economic terms_

The art market can feel daunting from the outside. The term itself sounds like a monolith — impenetrable, opaque, reserved for a particular kind of buyer. In reality it is a convening subset of unique players, submarkets, and motivations that, once you understand them, begin to look a great deal like every other alternative asset class. With one meaningful exception.

In art, the heart can outbid the spreadsheet. And it does so regularly, in rooms that set global records, and in the quieter secondary transactions that never make the news.

This concept has a name: the emotional premium.

1. The Emotional Premium_

How one arrives at the premium varies depending on the artwork, the artist, the time of sale, and the buyer themselves. There are very real moments within the art world where purchases are made well beyond the seemingly reasonable valuation of an artwork.

Once such a record price is paid — particularly when it carries a high emotional premium — the likelihood of finding another buyer in the short or medium term with both a similar emotional connection to the piece and the financial means to purchase it becomes exceedingly slim. This holds true even if the work were to return to the market at the same price, let alone attempting to profit from its resale.

In essence, despite the extensive media attention they garner, record-breaking works often turn out to be less than ideal financial investments. Not because they lack value, but because they carry so much of it — of a kind that cannot easily be transferred.

1.1 The Look of Dr. Gachet_

Consider the groundbreaking 1990 sale of van Gogh's Portrait of Dr. Gachet at Christie's New York — which broke every record of its time, reaching $82.5 million. The buyer was Ryoei Saito, a Japanese paper-industry magnate.

During the auction, Saito's representative bid aggressively, following a directive to secure the painting at any cost. As described in the New York Times, Saito had seen the work two years earlier at the Metropolitan Museum of Art and — his own word — fell in love with it. His motivation was deeply personal: he believed the melancholic portrayal of Dr. Gachet resonated with his own emotional state.

The painting was not acquired because a model said it would appreciate. It was acquired because someone felt it. That feeling created the price. And the price, once set, became a data point that shaped how every subsequent valuation of comparable works was built.

This is the emotional premium in full expression.

2. Cultural Economics and What It Means for Investors_

Cultural economics is a field that examines art and culture not as peripheral to the economy but as active participants in it. Cultural commodities — artworks among them — are notable for their dual significance: they possess both artistic and economic value. These two registers do not always move together.

A key concept here is cultural capital — the reservoir of knowledge, awareness, and historical context that individuals or societies hold. Cultural capital shapes what a work means, to whom, and for how long. It is what makes a Basquiat worth more in 2026 than it was when he was alive, and what made his work nearly unsellable in certain markets during his lifetime.

For investors: the shift from physical to digital has extended the purview of cultural economics considerably. What was previously confined to physical assets now includes the cultural and economic importance of the internet age — provenance, authentication, and community in new forms. The NFT market tested this at scale and demonstrated both the reality of digital cultural capital and the speed at which it can collapse when community consensus shifts.

3. What the Art Market Actually Is_

The global art market is not one market. It is a network of auction houses, dealers, galleries, advisors, agents, individual collectors, museums, public institutions, and service providers engaged in the purchase and sale of unique and collectible works. Each submarket is defined by artists and genres — and they move with different cycles, different participants, and different liquidity profiles.

According to the 2023 UBS Art Market Report, global art sales in 2022 reached an estimated $67.8 billion — a 3% year-on-year increase that surpassed pre-pandemic levels. But it is important to note that not all segments experienced uniform gains. The market's headline number conceals significant dispersion between blue-chip and emerging artist categories, between primary and secondary market activity, and between public auction results and private sales, which remain opaque almost by design.

Similar to private equity, art's relative lack of transparency can make it look unattainable from the outside. It is not. But it does reward the investor who comes prepared with vocabulary and framework before entering.

4. Questions to Hold Before Investing in Art_

  • Is this a purchase or an investment — and am I honest with myself about which one it is?
  • What is the provenance of this work, and has it been independently verified?
  • What is the liquidity profile — how would I exit, and at what expected discount to current valuation?
  • Is the artist living or deceased, represented or estate-held, and how does that affect market access?
  • What emotional premium am I paying — and is it mine, or am I inheriting someone else's?
