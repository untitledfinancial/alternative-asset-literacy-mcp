The Rails Were Built Without Us_

It is important to recognize that our current financial structures and institutions were born in an era when women had no legal standing to participate in them. JPMorgan/Chase, Barclays, Citi, and Bank of America were all founded and operating before women in the United States could cast a vote. The rails of our financial products rest in that upheaval. Understanding what we inherited is the first step to building beyond it.

🏹 The 20th Century_

Outside of the Industrial Revolution that brought many women and children into the workforce, a large portion of 19th and early 20th century economic progress was the expansion of the American West.

It was the 1839 Married Women's Property Acts that first allowed women to own property, engage in business, and legally keep their own income. This was a foundational shift — stating women were no longer their husband's property, nor did their inheritance automatically become their spouse's. Yet it was still not commonly practiced.

Women were not included in the United States Constitution as citizens. In her letters to her husband, Abigail Adams contributed perspective that was eventually ignored — but showed the early need for women's equanimity within a system designed around their absence.

Women have historically built the American Economy through both paid and unpaid labor. Yet until very recently they had little ability to determine their own economic fate.

Even as the Industrial Revolution brought great financial power to the United States, women were not given the opportunity to cast a vote — the only structure for representation — until 1920, when the Nineteenth Amendment was ratified. Globally, the United Nations General Assembly adopted the Convention on Political Rights of Women in 1954.

Why bring this up?

Because the multi-national banking conglomerates that still define our financial infrastructure — in their formation, family-run businesses — were founded and scaling during this period. The Industrial Revolution set the foundation for how our financial infrastructure still operates today.

Imagine all the social, political, and economic forces at play during the turn of the century. The battle for equality, voting rights, access to education, and the power to decide one's path — colliding. These are the underpinnings of our current economic and financial infrastructure. The rails of our financial products rest in this upheaval.

Mid Century_

The 1950s and 60s brought industrialization, cheaper goods, and the Post-War Boom. Women were pursuing education — but few were allowed to fully flex those muscles in the workforce.

Betty Friedan's The Feminine Mystique (1963) pointed out what economists had not quantified: through history, men had been able to write and control their destinies. Finances were a man's game. Women were relegated to the outskirts of society, far from power, and far from having the pleasure of dictating laws, regulations, and industries that affected their daily lives.

Many of you felt the effects of 2008. The Mortgage Backed Security — first brought to market in 1968 — became the vehicle that, through deregulation and misaligned incentives, brought an entire industry to its knees. Many financial products utilized today have the same blueprint.

From the late 1970s to 2020, American business and industry radically changed. Significant deregulation starting in the Carter Administration and continuing through Reagan, guided by the free-market economics of Nobel laureate Milton Friedman, moved policy away from Roosevelt's New Deal. The majority of the SEC's core regulations were formed in 1934.

Here We Are Now_

During the Obama Administration, in order to update laws and promote broader access to financial products — including alternative assets — the SEC added Regulation Crowdfunding (CF) and Regulation A. As of November 2020, a new set of regulatory laws lowered the threshold of "accredited" investors and broadened the amounts companies are able to raise.

The Dodd-Frank Act introduced updated consumer and market protections to offset the conditions that led to 2008. Hopefully there will continue to be a balance within the financial system to prevent another Black Swan event.

In the context of today — with the world's wealthiest men competing to beat each other into outer space — we have a cultural reckoning. In the wake of multiple overlapping cultural movements, there is still much work to be done. Not only in the hands of women, but in the founders of the platforms who will serve them.

Women as a whole understand the needs of the collective. Of society. Of future generations.

Every woman should read A Timeline of Women's Legal History in the United States by Stanford Law Professor Cunnea — a thorough and broad accounting of the legal components within U.S. legislation that shaped the financial landscape we navigate now.

Human history is complex. So is being a woman. May this be taken with the hope that there is a better way forward. Now, more than ever, we need to rebuild and retool financial instruments that meet the needs of all — not just the few. Technology and updated SEC regulations now allow for a clearer, more optimized, and more financially inclusive path forward.
