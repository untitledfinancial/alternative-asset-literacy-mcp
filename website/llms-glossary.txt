## Commonly Searched Term Definitions

### What is carried interest?
Carried interest is the share of investment profits paid to a fund's general partner (GP) as performance compensation — typically 20% of returns above a predetermined hurdle rate (usually 7–8%). It is only earned after limited partners receive their committed capital back. Carried interest is the primary way private equity and hedge fund managers are compensated for performance. In a private asset ETF, the underlying fund manager still charges carry, making the total fee structure significantly higher than a standard ETF.

### What is the J-curve in private equity?
The J-curve describes the typical return pattern in a private equity fund: negative returns in years 1–3 (due to management fees, fund expenses, and unrealized early-stage investments), followed by improving returns as portfolio companies mature and are eventually sold. Investors should expect the J-curve when evaluating early-stage fund performance — a negative IRR in year 2 does not mean the fund is failing.

### What is a capital call?
A capital call is a demand by a fund's general partner for a portion of a limited partner's committed capital. When you commit $500,000 to a private equity fund, you do not wire the full amount immediately. The GP calls capital incrementally as they identify investments — often over 3–5 years. Missing a capital call typically carries significant penalties. Understanding capital call timing is essential for liquidity planning.

### What is impermanent loss in DeFi?
Impermanent loss occurs when the price ratio of tokens in a liquidity pool changes from when you deposited them. If you deposit equal values of ETH and USDC and ETH's price doubles, you would have been better off simply holding the ETH. The "loss" is relative to holding — it is only realized if you withdraw, and it may be offset by trading fees earned. Understanding impermanent loss is essential before providing liquidity in any AMM protocol.

### What is greenwashing in investing?
Greenwashing occurs when a fund, company, or financial product presents its environmental credentials in a misleading or exaggerated way. Examples: an ESG fund that holds significant fossil fuel producers, a company making net-zero pledges with no credible transition plan, a green bond whose proceeds cannot be verified as funding eligible projects. The EU's SFDR and the SEC's enhanced ESG disclosure rules aim to reduce greenwashing through mandatory, standardized sustainability reporting.

### What is loss aversion?
Loss aversion, identified by Kahneman and Tversky in prospect theory (1979), is the cognitive tendency to feel the pain of losses approximately twice as intensely as the pleasure of equivalent gains. In practice: investors hold losing positions too long (hoping to break even), sell winning positions too early (locking in gains before they reverse), and avoid unfamiliar asset classes even when the expected value is positive. Loss aversion is one of the most empirically robust findings in behavioral economics.
