The Myth, The Snowflake, and the Data_

There have been many myths over centuries that have defined both social and interpersonal factors around women and finances. Notice: not "investing" — finances. Because first we need to break down the foundational myths that we, as a collective society, have embedded into our psyche around the natural interests, barriers, and ownership of money.

💨 The Myth

The fear around your ability to invest — in all aspects — should be countered within the proper framework of facts, not societal projections.

Common misconceptions. Examples of what gets projected onto women:

  • A one-size-fits-all approach to wealth.
  • Women are afraid to take financial risks.
  • Women are uninterested in investing.

None of these hold up against the data. More on that below.

❄️ The Snowflake

We are conditioned to accept — unknowingly or begrudgingly — others' definitions of us. This happens in every aspect of life, but shows itself most in the area of money.

Genetically, we are all unique creatures with the same foundational structure. Our ability to pattern match, future project, and navigate systems — both natural and man-made — allows for our capacity to build the life we desire. We show up in the world incredibly diverse and individualized.

Within the workforce, women have different career trajectories, goals, and retirement needs. If you are outside the traditional workforce, your needs are even more specialized. I understand. I have been in both scenarios.

Like cattle calls, being evaluated with distinct and unjust historical biases, we get plugged into a broken system. Then, when moments of failure come about, we become a working example of those broken systems. A mirror. These broken examples reflect mass social and infrastructure failings projected onto the female investor.

It is better to move away from a binary thought process of (+ / −) toward an intuitive ecosystem — looking at multiple factors in financial wellness, outside of just money in / money out.

The way our parents and grandparents navigated wealth creation does not exist in the same way for current investors. The rules of this new terrain are currently being written.

The Brain and Money_

Outside of learned behavior, neuroscientists and psychologists have found that people do not always behave the way economists describe.

Within the study of Neuroeconomics, Dr. Jonathan Cohen, co-director at the Princeton Neuroscience Institute, breaks down two terms in decision making: "deliberative" and "emotional." He describes the seemingly subtle differences that are acutely fundamental in how we make choices — especially under conditions of uncertainty or scarcity.

Different parts of our brain have adapted to different types of circumstances. Currently there is not a control structure that always knows the most appropriate choice in any given circumstance. It is important to know that when we make investing decisions, we are working with the best information we have at that moment.

Moving forward: utilize your intuition versus "gut" reaction. They are not the same thing.

The idea of perfection will not help in this situation. The key to this journey is learning how to fully trust one's own investing process.

💾 The Data Drivers

The data on women and investing is not what the myths suggest.

Source: Merrill Lynch / Age Wave, "Women & Financial Wellness: Beyond the Bottom Line" (2018)

These numbers tell a clear story. Women want to invest. The barrier is not interest or aptitude — it is access to the right framework. That is what knowledge can change.

Women want their relationship with money to be linked to their values, goals, and priorities. That preference is not a weakness in the system. It is a signal that the current system is not meeting the need.

Financial Embodiment_

The term "embodiment" is a great avenue for personal integration. Financial wellness is imperative to navigate all the twists and turns life offers. It provides a point of preparation as life unfolds.

Two questions worth sitting with:

  • What is my Baseline Readiness? — Have I taken the steps to prepare for a range of financial outcomes?
  • What is my Baseline Resilience? — If something goes sideways, how long can I hold?

These terms come from disaster preparedness. They translate directly to portfolio thinking. Observing, planning, and acting — not from fear, but from information — is the foundation.

Elizabeth Gilbert spoke on the concept of "no fear" being for sociopaths. We all need a healthy amount of fear. It keeps us alive, keeps us aware, and keeps us moving forward. Healthy fear is based on the truth of the actual situation — not blind faith that all things will magically work out.

Personal Agency_

Integral investing — one of personal agency — is recovering or uncovering your sense of power. Especially for women, the topic of money, finances, and economic empowerment is mixed with a complex history around the roles and value of women.

More than spreadsheets and data points, psychology plays a massive role in how you interact with money.

The data is not against you. The myths are. And myths can be replaced.
