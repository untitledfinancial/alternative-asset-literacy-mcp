Women & Wealth 8 min read

Who We Are vs. Why We Are Here_

Never before in the history of humankind have women had such a megaphone for personal choice. Yet the financial structures built to serve them — the models, the products, the advisory relationships — reflect a poor optimization of that voice and that power. This is not an accident. It is an inheritance. And like any inheritance, it can be examined, questioned, and redirected.

This is not a call to overhaul everything. Before you start, recognize that your financial journey does not need a complete reinvention — rather, small, informed tweaks along the way. If you arrive at a path that feels unfamiliar, keep walking, testing each stone. The footing is there. It might just need some reinforcement.

And you are not alone in this.

1. The Myth, the Snowflake, and the Data_

There have been myths over centuries that have defined both social and interpersonal dynamics around women and finances. Notice the word is finances, not investing — because first we must examine the foundational assumptions embedded into our collective psyche around the natural interests, barriers, and ownership of money itself.

We are conditioned, often without knowing it, to accept others' definitions of us. This happens across every dimension of life, but it shows itself most directly in relation to money. From here, consider your own emerging individuality — especially in connection to your financial future. Genetically we are all unique, yet share the same foundational structure. Our capacity to pattern-match, project forward, and navigate both natural and man-made systems gives us exactly what is needed to build the financial life we actually want.

"Women so often carry this delusion of being innately wrong, of needing to be numbed, controlled, caged or hidden — a delusion smuggled into us in the Trojan horse of a thousand old stories."
— Catherine Lacey

In the context of current social structures, one may feel that personal responsibility outweighs the relevance of systemic bias. While radical personal responsibility matters, it would be an injustice not to also name what is outside your control. There are systems of oppression, marginalization, and institutional inequity that exist independent of individual effort. You deserve financial instruments — and financial literacy — that account for that reality.

2. The Pay Penalty and the Portfolio Gap_

Many women have been in the workforce since their teens, navigating structures that make it difficult to naturally grow wealth when earning $0.80 on the dollar — and that figure is a baseline that drops further for women of color. The pay gap is not a myth or a rounding error. It is a compounding structural disadvantage that affects how much can be saved, invested, and grown over a lifetime.

And yet: as of 2019, 42% of all businesses and firms in the U.S. were women-owned. One in five firms with revenue of $1 million or more is female-owned. The IFC estimates that women-owned businesses face a $1.7 trillion global financing gap — not because they are less creditworthy, but because the financial products and advisory relationships they encounter were not designed with them in mind.

Women also live longer than men, on average. This is not a footnote — it is one of the most consequential financial facts in existence. A longer life means a longer drawdown period, more exposure to healthcare costs, greater vulnerability to inflation's long-term erosion, and, critically, more time for compound growth to work in your favor if you start. The structural case for women to engage deeply with alternative assets — assets that can provide uncorrelated returns, inflation protection, and long-horizon upside — is unusually strong.

3. How Women Enter Wealth_

There is no single path. Some women invest from earned income, navigating corporate structures that have not always made it easy. Some enter through entrepreneurship. Some through inheritance — family money passed down through the fortitude of generations past. Some become widows. Some navigate divorce, with alimony or asset division as the starting point of a new financial chapter.

If you find yourself at any of these entry points, the goal is the same: to move from whatever position you are in toward greater financial fluency, greater ownership of your decisions, and greater alignment between your capital and your values.

Multiple avenues lead to the same destination. Financial freedom and long-term wealth is the journey. The entry point is wherever you are right now.

4. Generational Gains_

Consider the narratives you have been handed about wealth. Consider the ecosystems you have found yourself within, financially speaking. Consider whether it is possible to step into a new thought process about wealth creation — one that accounts for your actual life, not a financial planning model designed for someone else's.

If the answer is yes, you are in the right place.

Asset allocation — having multiple financial assets working on your behalf simultaneously — is one of the most important yet easily overlooked components of financial freedom. It creates the conditions for choice. Understanding where alternative assets fit in that picture, and which ones are available to you regardless of accreditation status, is the work.
