# What Is an Accredited Investor?

An accredited investor is an individual or entity that meets specific financial thresholds set by the SEC, granting access to certain private investment opportunities. But understanding what accredited status means — and what it doesn't — matters whether you qualify or not. Many of today's most significant investment categories, including DeFi, ESG funds, and climate investing, are fully accessible to all investors. Knowing the landscape helps you understand what's available to you right now and what may open up as your financial picture changes.

---

## The Short Answer

Under current SEC rules, an individual qualifies as an accredited investor if they meet **any one** of the following:

- **Income:** Earned more than $200,000 in each of the last two years (or $300,000 with a spouse/partner), and expect to do the same this year
- **Net worth:** Has a net worth exceeding $1 million, alone or with a spouse — *excluding* the value of a primary residence
- **Professional credentials:** Holds a Series 7, Series 65, or Series 82 license in good standing
- **Knowledgeable employee:** Is a knowledgeable employee of the private fund they are investing in

Entities (trusts, LLCs, family offices) have separate qualification criteria based on assets under management or ownership structure.

---

## Why Accredited Status Matters

Most alternative investments — private equity, hedge funds, venture capital, private credit, certain real estate syndicates — are only legally available to accredited investors. Sponsors are not permitted to offer these securities publicly without full SEC registration (which would be cost-prohibitive for most private funds).

Accredited status unlocks private equity, hedge funds, and certain structured vehicles. But a significant portion of the alternative investment universe — DeFi protocols, public ESG funds, climate ETFs, gender lens funds, and art-backed vehicles — is available to all investors. You do not need accredited status to start learning or to participate in many of the categories Alternative Asset Literacy covers.

---

## What Changes When You Qualify

**Access expands significantly:**

- Private equity and venture capital funds
- Hedge fund subscriptions
- Private real estate syndications and opportunity zone funds
- Regulation D (Reg D) offerings
- Private placements and SPACs before public listing
- Certain art funds and collectible investment vehicles
- Private credit and direct lending funds

**What does not change:**

Accredited status does not mean an investment is suitable or well-priced. The SEC exemption from registration means these products receive less regulatory scrutiny, not more credibility. Due diligence responsibility shifts entirely to the investor.

---

## Qualified Purchaser: The Next Tier

Beyond accredited investor, a **qualified purchaser** is an individual or family-owned business owning at least $5 million in investments. Certain hedge funds and private funds are restricted to qualified purchasers only — a narrower, higher-threshold category.

| Designation | Threshold | Access |
|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Retail investor | No minimum | Public securities only |
| Accredited investor | $1M net worth or $200K income | Reg D offerings, private funds |
| Qualified purchaser | $5M in investments | Largest hedge funds, certain private funds |

---

## A Note on the K-Curve

The accredited investor threshold is frequently cited as a driver of the K-shaped economic recovery. Because private market returns have outpaced public market returns in several asset class cycles, access to those returns has been structurally limited to households already above the wealth threshold. There is ongoing regulatory debate about whether the income/net worth thresholds should be replaced by financial sophistication tests — which is why the SEC added the professional credentials pathway in 2020.
