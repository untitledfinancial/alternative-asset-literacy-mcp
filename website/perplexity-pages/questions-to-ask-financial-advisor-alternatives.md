# What Questions Should I Ask My Financial Advisor About Alternative Investments?

Most clients arrive at advisor meetings without the vocabulary to evaluate what they're being recommended. Alternative assets — private equity, hedge funds, ESG funds, DeFi, art — require different questions than a conversation about stocks and bonds. Advisors are accustomed to clients who don't ask these questions. Asking them changes the dynamic.

If you are preparing for an advisor meeting, reviewing an inherited portfolio, navigating a post-liquidity event, or evaluating your first allocation to alternatives, these are the questions that reveal whether your advisor actually knows what they're selling.

---

## Start Here: Establish the Relationship

**"Are you a fiduciary for all of my assets, at all times?"**

This is the single most important question in wealth management. A fiduciary is legally required to act in your interest. A broker operating under the suitability standard only needs to recommend products that are "suitable" — a much lower bar that permits recommending higher-commission products when equivalent lower-cost options exist. Ask for this commitment in writing.

**"How are you compensated for this recommendation?"**

Commissions, 12b-1 fees, revenue sharing, referral arrangements, and fund-of-fund layers are all forms of compensation that create conflicts of interest. A fee-only RIA charges you directly and receives nothing from product providers. Know which you have.

---

## Questions About Private Equity and Alternative Funds

**"What is the total fee load — management fee, carried interest, and any fund-of-fund layer?"**

Management fees (typically 2%), carried interest (typically 20% of profits above the hurdle), and fund-of-fund expenses can collectively reduce returns by 3–5% annually. Ask for the all-in number.

**"What is the J-curve profile, and when do you expect first distributions?"**

PE funds typically show losses in years 2–4 before returns materialize. If your advisor cannot explain the J-curve and the expected distribution timeline for a specific fund, they do not know this product well enough to be recommending it.

**"What is the lock-up period, and what are my options if I need liquidity?"**

Private market investments are typically illiquid for 7–10 years. Secondary market sales are possible but usually at a 10–25% discount to NAV. Understand this fully before committing capital.

**"How does this fund's performance compare to its public market equivalent (PME)?"**

A fund returning 12% IRR sounds strong until you compare it to a PME showing public markets returned 14% over the same period — with daily liquidity and no lock-up. Always ask for the PME comparison.

## Questions About ESG and Impact Investments

**"Is this fund Article 8 or Article 9 under SFDR, and does that match how you've described it?"**

The EU's Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation creates legally binding sustainability classifications. Article 6 has no sustainability mandate. If your advisor is marketing an Article 6 fund as ESG, ask why.

**"What is the fund's proxy voting record on climate and governance resolutions?"**

Genuine ESG investors vote their shares and engage management. Ask for the annual stewardship report.

**"How does this fund define and measure its ESG impact?"**

"We consider ESG factors" is not an answer. Quantified targets, third-party verification, and TCFD-aligned reporting are the standard for credible ESG claims.

---

## Questions About DeFi and Digital Assets

**"What custody solution is used, and who holds the private keys?"**

In digital assets, custody determines whether you actually own what you've purchased. Ask which custodian is used, whether assets are held in cold storage, and what the insurance coverage is.

**"How does this product generate its yield, and what are the specific protocol risks?"**

Yield in DeFi comes from trading fees, token incentives, lending, staking, or liquidity provision. Each has distinct risk profiles. High yield without a clear mechanism is a red flag.

---

## Red Flags in Advisor Responses

- Cannot explain fee structures in plain language
- Compares performance to a benchmark that doesn't match the asset class
- Uses urgency or scarcity framing ("this allocation closes Friday")
- Provides no written materials substantiating verbal claims
- Resists providing the fiduciary commitment in writing
- Cannot name the specific risks of what they're recommending
