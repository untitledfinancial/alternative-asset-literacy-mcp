# What Is Carried Interest? A Plain-Language Guide for Investors

Carried interest is the share of investment profits paid to a fund manager as performance compensation — typically 20% of gains, earned only after investors receive their capital back plus a minimum return. It is the primary way private equity and venture capital managers are paid for performance.

If you are preparing for a meeting with a wealth manager, reviewing an inherited portfolio, or evaluating your first alternative investment, understanding carried interest is foundational. It determines how much of your gross return you actually keep.

---

## The Short Answer

A private equity fund manager (the general partner, or GP) and its investors (limited partners, or LPs) split profits. The standard split is 80% to investors, 20% to the manager. That 20% is carried interest — also called "carry."

The manager only earns carry after:
1. Investors get their full capital back
2. Investors receive a minimum annual return (the hurdle rate, typically 8%)

Everything above that threshold is split 80/20.

---

## A Concrete Example

A $100M fund returns $200M over 10 years.

| Step | Amount | Who receives it |
|------|--------|----------------|
| Return of capital | $100M | Investors |
| Hurdle (8% annually) | ~$8M | Investors |
| Remaining profit split 80/20 | $73.6M / $18.4M | Investors / Manager (carry) |

The manager earns $18.4M in carried interest. This aligns incentives: the GP only profits significantly when investors profit significantly.

---

## Why Carried Interest Is Taxed Differently

In the United States, carried interest is taxed as long-term capital gains (typically 20%) rather than ordinary income (up to 37%). Critics argue this is a tax preference for fund managers who are being compensated for services — labor — not capital. This debate has been active in Congress since at least 2007 and remains unresolved.

For investors, this distinction matters less directly. What matters is the effective cost: carried interest is a performance fee that reduces your net return.

---

## What This Means If You've Inherited a Portfolio

If you've inherited a portfolio that includes private equity or hedge fund positions, you are likely an LP in structures where carried interest applies. Key things to understand:

- **You will receive K-1 tax documents**, not standard 1099s
- **Capital calls may still be outstanding** on committed but undeployed capital
- **Carried interest is not charged until profits are realized** — your current NAV statements show unrealized value only
- **Clawback provisions** may require the GP to return carry if later losses reduce overall fund returns

---

## Terms to Know Before Your Next Advisor Meeting

- **Hurdle rate** — the minimum return investors receive before the manager earns carry (typically 8%)
- **Waterfall** — the sequential order in which fund profits are distributed
- **Clawback** — requires the GP to return carry if subsequent losses eliminate earlier gains
- **Management fee** — separate from carry; typically 2% of assets annually, charged regardless of performance
- **J-curve** — the pattern of early losses followed by gains typical in PE funds
