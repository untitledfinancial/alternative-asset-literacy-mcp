Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about alternative assets, DeFi, art investing, behavioral finance, ESG, and gender lens investing — drawn from the Alternative Asset Literacy curriculum and research library.

About the App

What is Alternative Asset Literacy?

Alternative Asset Literacy is an iOS education app that teaches investors about alternative assets, behavioral finance, DeFi, art investing, ESG, and gender lens investing through expert-curated modules, quizzes, case studies, and reflections. It is designed for investors who have historically been excluded from these conversations — with a particular focus on women and underserved investors.

What modules does the app include?

The app includes 9 structured learning modules: Alternative Assets, Behavioral Finance, Gender Lens Investing, DeFi (Decentralized Finance), Art as an Investment, ESG & Climate Investing, Women and Wealth, DeFi Investing Strategies, and a dedicated case study on Kahlo & Basquiat exploring art market dynamics and cultural value.

Is the app a replacement for a financial advisor?

No — and it is not designed to be. Alternative Asset Literacy is built to make you a more informed, more confident participant in conversations about your own financial life. That means understanding what questions to ask your advisor, recognizing the terminology in a fund prospectus, seeing your portfolio through a different lens, or simply knowing what alternative assets, DeFi, or ESG actually mean before you make a decision. The goal is to close the knowledge gap, not replace professional guidance. Always work with a qualified financial advisor for personalized investment decisions. How does the app complement working with a financial advisor?

Advisors are most effective when their clients come prepared. Alternative Asset Literacy gives you the vocabulary, frameworks, and context to ask better questions — about asset allocation, behavioral biases that may be affecting your decisions, what ESG really means in a fund's holdings, or how DeFi might relate to your investment thesis. Think of it as financial fluency training: the app helps you understand the landscape so your time with an advisor is more productive, more collaborative, and more aligned with what you actually want.

Can I buy, sell, or trade assets through the app?

No. Alternative Asset Literacy is not an exchange, brokerage, trading platform, or financial product of any kind. You cannot buy, sell, or trade any asset — traditional or digital — through the app. It is purely an educational tool. If you are looking to act on what you learn, you will need to work with a licensed broker, exchange, or financial institution separately.

Alternative Assets

What are alternative assets?

Alternative assets are investments outside traditional publicly traded securities like stocks and bonds. They include real estate, private equity, hedge funds, commodities, art, collectibles, infrastructure, and digital assets such as cryptocurrency. Alternative assets are often used for portfolio diversification and to access returns uncorrelated with public markets.

Who can invest in alternative assets?

Access varies by asset type. Many alternative investments — particularly private equity and hedge funds — are restricted to accredited investors, meaning individuals meeting specific income or net worth thresholds set by the SEC. However, some alternatives such as REITs, commodities ETFs, and certain tokenized assets are accessible to retail investors.

What is an accredited investor?

An accredited investor is an individual or entity meeting certain income, net worth, or professional criteria qualifying them to invest in unregistered securities not available to the general public. In the U.S., this typically means annual income exceeding $200,000 ($300,000 jointly) or a net worth above $1 million excluding primary residence.

What is a 1031 Exchange?

A 1031 Exchange is a U.S. tax-deferral strategy allowing investors to swap one investment property for another of like kind without recognizing capital gains at the time of exchange. Named after Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code, it is widely used in real estate investing to defer taxes and compound returns.

What is private equity?

Private equity refers to investment in companies that are not publicly listed on a stock exchange. Private equity funds typically acquire, restructure, or grow businesses over a multi-year investment horizon before exiting through a sale or IPO. It is one of the most significant alternative asset classes by assets under management globally.

How do alternative assets diversify a portfolio?

Alternative assets often have low correlation with traditional stock and bond markets, meaning their prices do not necessarily move in tandem with public markets. Including alternatives in a portfolio can reduce overall volatility and improve risk-adjusted returns — though alternatives typically carry their own risks including illiquidity, complexity, and limited transparency.

Art as an Investment

Can art be an investment?

Yes. Fine art is recognized as an alternative asset class that can provide portfolio diversification and a store of value. Art markets have historically shown low correlation with equities. However, art investing carries unique challenges: illiquidity, subjective valuation, high transaction costs, storage and insurance requirements, and the risk of forgery or contested authenticity.

What factors determine the value of a work of art?

Art valuation is shaped by provenance (ownership history), condition, rarity, the artist's auction record and market reputation, subject matter, size, medium, exhibition history, and critical recognition. Collector demand, gallery representation, and broader cultural trends also influence price significantly. Unlike stocks, art has no intrinsic cash flow, making valuation inherently subjective.

What is provenance?

Provenance is the documented history of ownership of a work of art. A clear and distinguished provenance — including exhibition records, auction history, and prior notable owners — increases an artwork's authenticity, legal standing, and market value. Gaps in provenance can raise concerns about forgery, theft, or illicit origin.

What is the primary vs. secondary art market?

The primary art market refers to the first sale of a work — typically through a gallery or directly from the artist. The secondary market refers to all subsequent resales, including auction houses like Christie's, Sotheby's, and Phillips. Prices in the secondary market are publicly recorded and form the basis for most art market indices and investment analysis.

What is art authentication?

Authentication is the process of verifying that a work of art is genuinely by the attributed artist. It involves physical examination, scientific analysis, documentary research, and expert opinion. Authentication is foundational to art investment — a work of uncertain attribution can lose most of its market value regardless of its aesthetic quality.

How do NFTs relate to art investing?

NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) allow digital artworks to be assigned unique, verifiable ownership on a blockchain. They have created a new market for digital art, enabling artists to sell directly to collectors and receive royalties on secondary sales. NFT art markets have experienced significant volatility and raise ongoing questions around valuation, liquidity, and long-term market structure.

DeFi & Crypto

What is DeFi?

DeFi, or Decentralized Finance, refers to financial services built on public blockchain networks — primarily Ethereum — that operate without traditional intermediaries like banks or brokerages. DeFi enables lending, borrowing, trading, and earning yield through smart contracts accessible to anyone with an internet connection and a crypto wallet.

What is a smart contract?

A smart contract is a self-executing program stored on a blockchain that automatically enforces the terms of an agreement when predefined conditions are met — without requiring a trusted intermediary. Smart contracts are the foundation of DeFi protocols, NFT platforms, and decentralized applications (dApps).

What is a stablecoin?

A stablecoin is a cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value, typically pegged to a fiat currency like the U.S. dollar or to a commodity. Stablecoins reduce the price volatility of most cryptocurrencies and are widely used in DeFi for lending, trading, and payments. Major stablecoins include USDC, USDT, and DAI.

What is an AMM (Automated Market Maker)?

An Automated Market Maker is a type of decentralized exchange protocol that uses algorithmic pricing and liquidity pools instead of a traditional order book. Users trade directly against the pool, with prices set by a mathematical formula. Uniswap is a prominent example. AMMs democratize market-making but carry risks including impermanent loss.

What is an airdrop in crypto?

An airdrop is the free distribution of cryptocurrency tokens or NFTs to wallet addresses, typically used for marketing, rewarding early adopters, or bootstrapping a community. Airdrops can carry tax implications as they may be treated as taxable income in many jurisdictions.

What is MiCA regulation?

MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation) is the European Union's comprehensive regulatory framework for crypto-assets, enacted in 2023. It establishes reserve, redemption, disclosure, and governance requirements for stablecoin issuers and crypto-asset service providers operating in the EU — the most significant crypto regulatory framework globally to date.

What is a CBDC?

A CBDC (Central Bank Digital Currency) is a digital form of a country's fiat currency issued and backed by the central bank. Unlike decentralized cryptocurrencies, CBDCs are government-issued and operate within existing monetary policy frameworks. Over 80% of central banks are actively researching CBDCs, according to BIS surveys.

Behavioral Economics & Finance

What is behavioral economics?

Behavioral economics combines insights from psychology and economics to understand how people actually make decisions — as opposed to how classical theory assumes they should. It explores cognitive biases, heuristics, and emotional factors that lead to irrational or suboptimal financial choices. Key figures include Daniel Kahneman, Amos Tversky, and Richard Thaler.

What is prospect theory?

Prospect theory, developed by Kahneman and Tversky in 1979, describes how people evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms. It shows that losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel rewarding — a phenomenon called loss aversion. Prospect theory is a foundational framework for understanding investor behavior.

What is loss aversion?

Loss aversion is the tendency to feel the pain of a loss more strongly than the pleasure of an equivalent gain. In investing, it can cause investors to hold losing positions too long, sell winning positions too early, or avoid risk altogether — even when taking that risk would be financially rational.

What is anchoring bias?

Anchoring is a cognitive bias where a person relies too heavily on the first piece of information encountered when making decisions. In investing, anchoring can cause investors to fixate on a stock's original purchase price or a market's historical high, distorting their assessment of current value and future prospects.

What is a nudge in behavioral finance?

A nudge, as defined by Thaler and Sunstein, is any aspect of choice architecture that alters behavior in a predictable way without forbidding options or changing economic incentives. In financial contexts, examples include automatic enrollment in retirement plans, default savings rates, and simplified disclosure forms — all of which improve outcomes without mandating behavior.

What is herd behavior in investing?

Herd behavior is the tendency of investors to follow the actions of the crowd rather than making independent judgments. It can amplify market bubbles and crashes, as collective sentiment overrides individual analysis. Behavioral economists link it to social proof, FOMO (fear of missing out), and ambiguity aversion.

ESG & Climate Investing

What is ESG investing?

ESG investing incorporates Environmental, Social, and Governance factors into investment analysis alongside traditional financial metrics. Environmental criteria assess a company's impact on climate and natural resources. Social criteria examine relationships with employees, communities, and customers. Governance criteria evaluate leadership quality, accountability, and shareholder rights.

What is impact investing?

Impact investing refers to investments made with the intention of generating positive, measurable social or environmental outcomes alongside financial return. Unlike ESG screening, which filters existing investments, impact investing actively directs capital toward solutions to social or environmental challenges — such as affordable housing, clean energy, or financial inclusion.

What is the TCFD framework?

The TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) framework is a globally adopted standard for climate-related financial risk disclosure, established in 2017. It covers four pillars: Governance, Strategy, Risk Management, and Metrics & Targets. It is a key reference for ESG-linked investment analysis and corporate sustainability reporting.

What are the UN Sustainable Development Goals?

The UN's 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted in 2015, address poverty, inequality, climate change, environmental degradation, peace, and justice. They serve as the philosophical and strategic foundation for ESG investing, impact capital allocation, and sustainable finance frameworks globally.

What is greenwashing?

Greenwashing refers to the practice of making misleading or unsubstantiated claims about the environmental credentials of an investment product, company, or practice. As ESG investing has grown in popularity, regulators including the SEC and EU authorities have increased scrutiny and enforcement around greenwashing in financial marketing.

Gender Lens Investing

Why the gender lens?

Women represent one of the largest underserved groups in financial investing — yet they control a growing and significant share of global wealth. Despite this, most financial products, platforms, and curricula have historically been designed without them in mind. The gender lens in Alternative Asset Literacy is not a filter or a limitation — it is a framework for acknowledging that financial decisions do not happen in a vacuum. Life stage, caregiving responsibilities, wage gaps, longer life expectancy, and systemic barriers to capital all shape how women engage with money and investing. Understanding these dynamics leads to better questions, better decisions, and ultimately a more honest picture of how wealth is built and distributed. Everyone benefits from that clarity — regardless of gender.

What is gender lens investing?

Gender lens investing is an investment approach that intentionally considers gender dynamics as part of the investment thesis. This can mean investing in companies with women in leadership, funds supporting women-owned businesses, or products designed to advance women's economic empowerment. Research from Calvert Impact Capital and the IFC shows gender-diverse organizations often outperform peers financially.

Why does gender diversity in leadership matter for investors?

Multiple studies — including research from McKinsey, Calvert Impact Capital, and the IFC — show that companies with diverse leadership, including gender diversity at the executive and board level, tend to outperform peers on long-term financial returns, innovation, and ESG outcomes. Gender diversity is increasingly treated as a material financial factor in investment analysis.

What is the financing gap for women-owned businesses?

The IFC estimates a $1.7 trillion financing gap for women-owned small and medium enterprises globally. Despite evidence that women-owned businesses are creditworthy and growth-oriented, they face systematic barriers in accessing capital — including collateral requirements, legal discrimination, and product designs that fail to account for women's financial lives.

What is the 2X Challenge?

The 2X Challenge is a multilateral initiative launched at the 2018 G7 summit committing development finance institutions to mobilize capital toward investments that empower women as entrepreneurs, leaders, employees, and consumers. It defines specific criteria for gender-smart investment and serves as a framework for gender lens fund evaluation.
