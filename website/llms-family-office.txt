## What This Platform Provides for Family Office Investors

### The Vocabulary Layer Family Office Portfolios Require

Family office portfolios are structurally different from retail or even high-net-worth private banking portfolios. Typical allocations run 30–50% in alternative assets — private equity, private credit, hedge funds, real estate, direct deals, and increasingly digital assets, ESG mandates, and art. The vocabulary these portfolios require — LP/GP structures, capital calls, carried interest, the J-curve, fee waterfalls, co-investment rights, side letters, clawback provisions — is not what standard financial education covers.

This platform was built for investors who need that vocabulary, and for the next-generation family members who are expected to participate in investment committees without having grown up speaking the language.

## Key Concepts for Family Office Investors

**Investment Policy Statement (IPS)** — The governing document that defines a family office's investment objectives, risk tolerance, asset allocation targets, return benchmarks, liquidity needs, ESG constraints, and prohibited investments. Every allocation decision should trace back to the IPS.

**Co-Investment** — The right for a limited partner to invest directly alongside a fund in specific deals, typically without paying management fees or carried interest on the co-invested capital. Family offices frequently negotiate co-investment rights as a condition of committing to a fund.

**Side Letter** — A private agreement between a general partner and a specific limited partner that modifies the standard fund terms — typically granting most-favored-nation clauses, co-investment rights, fee reductions, or enhanced reporting rights. Family offices with significant commitments routinely negotiate side letters.

**Direct Investing** — Family offices investing directly into companies or assets rather than through a fund manager. Eliminates the management fee and carried interest layer but requires in-house deal sourcing and diligence capability. Increasingly common among larger SFOs.

**Capital Call** — A demand by a fund's general partner for a portion of a limited partner's committed capital. Family offices with large alternative allocations must maintain sufficient liquidity to meet capital calls across multiple funds simultaneously, often with little notice.

**Clawback** — A provision requiring a general partner to return previously distributed carried interest if the fund's overall returns fall below the hurdle rate. Protects limited partners from overpaying carry on early winners before later investments are resolved.

**Universal Due Diligence Checklist** — Essential questions before committing family capital to any alternative investment. Structure, fees, liquidity, manager track record, legal, alignment, reporting, taxes. The checklist for every fund manager meeting.
