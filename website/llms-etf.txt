## What Just Changed

In 2024, the SEC approved rule changes enabling registered investment funds — including ETF structures — to hold meaningful allocations in illiquid private assets. Private equity. Private credit. Infrastructure. Real assets. Asset classes that were previously accessible only to institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals through closed-end funds, limited partnerships, and multi-year lock-up structures with minimum investments of $250,000 or more.

Firms including BlackRock, Apollo Global Management, State Street, KKR, and Franklin Templeton have moved quickly to bring these structures to market. Bloomberg, the Wall Street Journal, and the Financial Times have called it one of the most significant structural shifts in the ETF industry in years.

If you have a brokerage account and you are paying attention, you are going to be offered one of these products. This platform exists so you know what you are looking at when that happens.

## What You Need to Understand Before You Invest

### What Private Equity Actually Is

Private equity means investing in companies that are not traded on any public stock exchange. You cannot buy them on Robinhood, Fidelity, or any brokerage app. The companies are owned privately — by buyout funds, growth equity funds, or venture capital firms — and investors access them through fund structures that pool capital from many investors.

Until 2024, the minimum to enter most private equity funds was $250,000 to $5,000,000, with a commitment period of 7–10 years. Your capital was locked in. You could not sell. You received capital calls — demands for portions of your committed capital as the fund identified investments.

The new ETF-adjacent structures aim to give retail investors exposure to these same assets in a more accessible, lower-minimum wrapper. But the underlying assets have not changed. They are still illiquid. Understanding how private equity works at the fund level is the prerequisite to evaluating whether any specific retail product makes sense for you.

The Alternative Investing module covers this from the beginning. It is available in full with a premium subscription.

### The Fee Problem

Private asset ETFs layer fees in a way that is entirely unlike a Vanguard index fund charging 0.03%.

You pay:
- The ETF or fund wrapper's expense ratio (typically 0.5–1.5%)
- The underlying private equity manager's management fee (typically 1.5–2% annually on committed capital)
- Carried interest — the fund manager's share of profits above the hurdle rate, typically 20%

These costs compound over time. A fund returning 12% gross may return 7–8% net after all fees — and that is before taxes. The difference matters enormously at scale.

The Universal Toolkit's Fee Breakdown by Asset Class walks through exactly how this works across private equity, private credit, hedge funds, and real assets. Understanding the total cost structure is not optional due diligence. It is the first due diligence.

### The Liquidity Question

Standard ETFs are liquid. You can sell any time the market is open. Private equity is illiquid — investments are locked up for years. The new structures use various mechanisms to bridge this gap: semi-liquid structures, interval fund mechanics, redemption queues, rolling liquidity windows, and cash buffers.

The critical question is what happens when markets decline and many investors want to sell at the same time. Redemption queues mean your request to exit may be processed over weeks or months. Cash buffers can be depleted. NAVs may not reflect true market stress in illiquid portfolios.

Understanding liquidity risk in these structures is not pessimism. It is basic due diligence for any investor who may need access to their capital.

### What Carried Interest Means for You

Carried interest is the share of investment profits paid to the fund manager — typically 20% of returns above a hurdle rate of 7–8%. It is how private equity managers are compensated for performance.

If a fund returns 15% in a year and the hurdle rate is 8%, the manager takes 20% of the 7% excess return. That is 1.4% of your invested capital — on top of the management fee. In strong years, carry is significant. Over a decade, the compounding effect on your net return is material.

When your advisor or fund marketing material shows you a projected or historical return, ask whether that number is gross or net of carried interest, management fees, and expenses. It almost always is not.

## The Behavioral Layer

The hardest part of investing in something new is not the complexity. It is the psychology.

Loss aversion makes unfamiliar asset classes feel riskier than familiar ones — even when the data doesn't support that. FOMO makes headline-grabbing new products feel more urgent than they should. Overconfidence — from reading a few Bloomberg articles — creates the illusion of understanding that can be expensive.

Seven interactive behavioral economics scenarios and a neuroscience Brain Map help you understand your own decision patterns before they cost you money. Grounded in Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory and Thaler's nudge research.

Knowing why you are tempted to buy something is as important as knowing what you are buying.
